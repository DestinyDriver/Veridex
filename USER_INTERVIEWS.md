# User Interviews

## Honest disclosure

**I did not complete three 10–15 minute interviews this week.** The brief is explicit that faking these is an instant reject, and the brief also says honesty scores higher than fake entries. So this file is the honest version.

Here's what I actually did, what I tried, and why I think it failed.

---

## What I attempted

Between **May 23 and May 27, 2026**, I cold-reached out to **~34 people** across four surfaces:

I had a template I iterated three times. The version I ended on:

> Hey [name] — building a small tool that audits a team's AI tool spend (Cursor, Copilot, Claude etc.) and recommends cheaper plans. Not selling anything. Looking for **15 minutes** to learn how you currently think about renewals for these tools. Free, no pitch, happy to share the audit results from your stack at the end. Any time this week work?

## Why I think it didn't land

Being honest with myself, three reasons — in order of how much I think each one mattered:

1. **No live link.** This is the big one. Every reply I got asked some version of "is there a demo?" Eng Ops people are _especially_ skeptical of unsolicited DMs without a working artefact behind them. I didn't deploy until day 7. Asking strangers for 15 minutes before having anything to show them put the burden of imagination on them, and most reasonable people don't accept that burden from a stranger.
2. **Wrong messenger.** I'm a college student with a tiny following. Cold DM credibility on X scales hard with the sender's profile. If a known Eng leader (or someone from Credex with their existing relationships) had sent the same template, the reply rate would probably have been 5–10× higher.
3. **Wrong timing.** I sent most DMs over a weekend / through the workweek of a US holiday period. Bad call.

## What I should have done instead

- **Built the smallest possible live demo first** — even just the form + a fake hardcoded result page — and deployed it on day 2, before any outreach. Then the cold DM has a link, and the link does the qualifying for me.
- **Started with my own network's second-degree connections.** Friends-of-friends are 10× more likely to give 15 minutes than strangers. I went straight to strangers because I thought it would scale faster. It didn't.
- **Offered something concrete in exchange** — "send me your tool list and I'll run the audit and email you the results before we talk" — instead of asking for time in exchange for a vague future benefit.
- **Posted in the communities I named in `GTM.md` (Rands, DX Slack, LeadDev)** instead of indie hackers, who are the wrong persona for this product. I picked Indie Hackers because I already had access. That's selection-by-convenience, which is the bias the brief is testing for.

## What this would have changed about the design (best guess)

I'm not going to claim insights from interviews I didn't have. But based on the **one half-conversation** I had with a college senior now working at a 40-person YC startup, who replied "send me the link when it's live," two things stand out:

- He didn't ask about Cursor or Copilot. He asked about **Vercel and Supabase bills** — those weren't on my radar as "AI tools" but they're adjacent in his mental model of "tools that keep getting more expensive." I'd consider broadening the audit's scope after launch.
- He said "I don't pick the plan, my CTO does, but I'm the one who notices the bill." That's a signal that the **buyer and the user are different people** — which would change who I market to (the noticer) vs. who I sell to (the decider). I haven't reflected this in the GTM doc yet.

That's one weak data point, not three interviews. I'm flagging it as such.
